Wenyuan Fan

UCSD/SIO/IGPP

Very low frequency earthquakes in between the seismogenic and tremor zones in Cascadia?


Megathrust earthquakes and their associated tsunamis cause some of the worst natural disasters. In addition to earthquakes, a wide range of slip behaviors are present at subduction zones, including slow earthquakes that span multiple orders of spatial and temporal scales. Understanding these events may shed light on the stress or strength conditions of the megathrust fault. Out of all types of slow earthquakes, very low frequency earthquakes (VLFEs) are most enigmatic because they are difficult to detect reliably, and the physical nature of VLFEs are poorly understood. Here we show three VLFEs in Cascadia that were dynamically triggered by a 2009 Mw 6.9 Canal de Ballenas earthquake in the Gulf of California. The VLFEs likely locate in between the seismogenic zone and the Cascadia episodic tremor and slip zone, including one event with a moment magnitude of 5.7. This is the largest VLFE reported to date, causing clear geodetic signals. Our results show that the Cascadia megathrust fault might slip rapidly at some spots in this gap zone, and such a permissible slip behavior has direct seismic hazard implications for coastal communities and perhaps further inland. Further, the observed seismic sources may represent a new class of slip events, whose characteristics do not fit current understandings of slow or regular earthquakes.

Nicolas DeSalvio

UCSD/SIO/IGPP

Ubiquitous earthquake dynamic triggering in southern California and Japan


Earthquakes can be dynamically triggered by the passing waves of other distant events. The frequent occurrence of dynamic triggering offers tangible hope in revealing earthquake nucleation processes. However, the physical mechanisms behind earthquake dynamic triggering have remained unclear, and contributions of competing hypotheses are challenging to isolate with individual case studies. To gain a systematic understanding of the spatiotemporal patterns of dynamic triggering, we first investigate the phenomenon in southern California from 2008 to 2017. We use the Quake Template Matching catalog and an approach that does not assume an earthquake occurrence distribution. We develop a new set of statistics to examine the significance of seismicity-rate changes as well as moment-release changes. Our results show that up to 70% of 1,388 global M≥6 events may have triggered earthquakes in southern California. The triggered seismicity often occurred several hours after the passing seismic waves. The Salton Sea Geothermal Field, San Jacinto fault, and Coso Geothermal Field are particularly prone to triggering. Although adjacent fault segments can be triggered by the same earthquakes, the majority of triggered earthquakes seem to be uncorrelated, suggesting that the process is primarily governed by local conditions and tectonic-scale processes are unlikely to significantly affect triggering occurrence. To further test the hypothesis, we investigate the crustal seismicity in Japan and observe similar triggering patterns, albeit at a reduced frequency. Intriguingly, the triggering rate increased significantly after the 2011 Tohoku-Oki earthquake, suggesting a possible shift of the stress field. The occurrence of dynamic triggering in southern California does not seem to correlate with ground motion (e.g., peak ground velocity) at the triggered sites, and we plan to comparatively investigate these waveform metrics in Japan. The current set of observations indicate that nonlinear, local processes may have primarily regulated the dynamic triggering cases.

Wing Ching Jeremy Wong

UCSD/SIO/IGPP

Variability in finite-fault slip models of the 2011 Tohoku-Oki earthquake


Large earthquakes can rupture over hundreds of kilometers within minutes. Finite-fault inversion resolves the spatial-temporal evolution of large earthquakes, thereby assisting in hazard mitigation. However, finite-fault inversion is often parameterized as an ill-conditioned problem with a large number of unknowns, suffering from non-uniqueness and uncertainties. Illustratively, over 40 finite-fault models have been published for the 2011 Mw 9.0 Tohoku-Oki earthquake, each depicting varying rupture processes. In this study, we systematically quantify the variability of the models and investigate the sensitivity of three types of data to the identified variabilities. We first project the models to the realistic megathrust geometry with a 1-km subfault size. We find that the models do not correlate well with each other at the scale regardless of the data types. However, the variability reduces when the models are downsampled to greater subfault sizes. Specifically, the models are in agreement at the 64 km subfault sizes, suggesting that their differences primarily arise from small-scale features. We then forward compute geodetic and teleseismic synthetics and compare them with the real observations. We find that seismic observations are sensitive to the rupture propagation, such as peak slip time at the subfaults, albeit neither teleseismic nor geodetic observations are sensitive to slip features less than 64 km. We also synthesize seafloor deformation due to the models and compare them with one obtained from tsunami data. The seafloor deformation synthetics correlate poorly, showing that they are sensitive to different slip features. This sensitivity is confirmed by synthetic seafloor deformation at the offshore S-net network using the suite of models. Our findings show that fine-scale slip features cannot be uniquely determined by remote or sparse observations, regardless of the data type, but are achievable by uniformly gridded dense offshore instrumentation.
